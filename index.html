Subsidized vs. unsubsidized: the rules

Direct Subsidized Loans — only for undergrads with financial need. The U.S. Department of Education pays the interest for you: while you're enrolled at least half-time, during the 6-month grace period after you leave school, and during authorized deferment periods. As long as you stay enrolled, these loans don't grow.

Direct Unsubsidized Loans — available to undergrad and grad students, no financial need required. Interest starts accruing the moment the loan is disbursed and keeps accruing the entire time you're in school, whether you pay it or not.

PLUS loans and private student loans — always accrue interest from disbursement, same as unsubsidized loans. No in-school interest subsidy applies.

Capitalization — any unpaid accrued interest on unsubsidized/PLUS/private loans gets added to your principal balance ("capitalized") at certain trigger points — typically when your grace period ends, or when a deferment/forbearance ends. After that, you pay interest on the new, larger balance. Paying at least the accruing interest each month — even while in school — prevents this and saves money over the life of the loan.

Grace period — most federal loans give you 6 months after you graduate or drop below half-time enrollment before payments are required.

Repayment plans — Standard (fixed payment over 10 years), Graduated, Extended, and Income-Driven plans (SAVE, IBR, PAYE) that base payments on income and family size. Which is cheapest depends on your future income and loan balance.

This is general educational information, not personalized financial or legal advice, and federal loan rules do change. Confirm current rules and your specific loan terms at studentaid.gov before making decisions.
